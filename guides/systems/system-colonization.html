System Colonisation Construction Guide

Series Systems Updated 2026-06-25
Briefing

Claim a dead system and build a home you own — permanently

01

What Colonisation Is

System Colonisation lets you, the Commander, claim an empty star system and build it into a real, permanent, inhabited part of the galaxy — a station, then a whole network of ports, settlements and installations that you designed and own.

For the entire history of Elite Dangerous, the inhabited galaxy — "the Bubble" — was static. Stations existed where Frontier placed them. You could shift faction influence, win wars, and fight Thargoids, but you could not create new infrastructure. That changed with the Trailblazers update (26 February 2025), which introduced colonisation as a player-driven way to make the Bubble grow for the first time.

The core loop is simple to state and large to execute:

  1. Claim a system. Pay a credit fee to claim an unpopulated system near existing space.
  2. Drop the beacon. Fly there and drop a Colonisation Beacon, which summons a giant Colonisation Ship.
  3. Haul the list. Ferry commodities — steel, composites, machinery, food, the lot — to that ship until a long shopping list is filled.
  4. Become Architect. Your first starport is built; you become the system's Architect.
  5. Keep building. From there: more ports, planetary settlements, mining outposts, research labs — each one shaping the system's economy, security, wealth and population.

It is, at heart, a logistics and construction activity wrapped around every other profession in the game. The hauling builds the bones; the choices you make about what to build decide what your system becomes — a mining hub, a tourist haven, a fortress, a trade nexus, or all of them at once.

Commander Note

Be clear-eyed going in: the building phase is trade- and hauling-heavy whatever you normally fly. The win is the finished system — a permanent base tuned to however you play (your own rings, RES sites, markets, missions, or a rearm point out in the black) once the cargo runs are behind you. Section 11 turns this into a concrete build plan.

02

Why It Matters & Who It's For

Colonisation is the single largest piece of player agency Frontier has ever shipped. Your colonised system becomes a genuine, persistent feature of the galaxy: it shows on the in-game galaxy map and is catalogued by third-party services like EDSM and INARA. Other Commanders can fly to it, dock at it, trade and fight in it. Your Commander name is stamped on the system as its Architect, effectively forever — the Architect designation does not change hands even if a rival Power later seizes political control.

Who should care

Profile A

The Builder

You want to leave a mark — a home system designed exactly how you like it, named, painted, and run on your terms. The deepest motivation, and the one Frontier built this for.

Profile B

The Logistician

You enjoy trade, hauling and route optimisation. Colonisation is the biggest hauling sink in the game and rewards efficient fleets and planning.

Profile C

The BGS / Power Player

A colony seeds a controllable faction into a new system. Useful for Background Simulation play and for pushing a chosen Power's footprint.

Profile D

The Specialist

Miner, combat pilot, passenger jockey or explorer who wants a system tuned to their profession — your own rings, your own RES, your own tourist beacons, your own rearm point in the black.

Reality Check

Colonisation is optional. Nothing forces you into it, and a single colony can absorb dozens of hours of hauling. It rewards patience and a large-cargo fleet. If you go in expecting a quick build, you'll bounce off it; go in treating it as a multi-week project and it's one of the most satisfying loops in the game.

The Payoffs

Permanence

Forever

A real system on the galaxy map, tagged with your name as Architect.

Architect Income

Weekly

A recurring credit payment from every system you control, scaled by the number and size of its facilities.

Designed Economy

Your Call

You decide whether the system mines, farms, manufactures, researches or fights.

Forward Base

Strategic

A home port, rearm/repair point and shipyard exactly where you want it.

Beyond the headline rewards, a finished colony pays off in ways tuned to how you play:

  • A base tuned to your profession. Pick a system with good planetary rings and the Resource Extraction Sites are yours — for mining or for combat bounties; build the matching economy and the markets, missions and security/conflict states follow.
  • A mission farm. Your founding faction generates missions. Keep it healthy via the BGS and you have a reliable source of combat, courier, trade and donation missions on tap — directly useful for any superpower's rank grind.
  • A trade engine. Build complementary economies (e.g. Extraction + Industrial + High-Tech) and you create profitable supply/demand inside one system or across a small cluster of your colonies.
  • Customisation. Facilities can be renamed (5 free random rolls, or a custom name for 5,000 ARX) and starport paint jobs apply like ship liveries.

Lexicon — Key Terms

Learn these eight terms and the rest of the manual reads easily.

TermWhat It Means
Colonisation ContactAn NPC service found in the Contacts panel of station services in populated space. Where you buy (and can cancel) a claim, and learn the basics.
Colonisation SuiteA module every ship now carries automatically (like the Discovery Scanner). It's what launches your beacon — you don't need to buy or fit it.
Colonisation BeaconThe object you drop at a preset spot (a flag icon on the system map) in your claimed system. Dropping it commits the claim and calls in the Colonisation Ship.
Colonisation Ship (CS)A capital-class vessel — bigger than a Fleet Carrier — that parks at your primary build site. You deliver the primary port's commodities to it. It leaves once the port is finished.
Primary PortThe first station in the system. Building it is what turns the system "live" and makes you the Architect. Cannot be demolished later.
System ArchitectYou, after the primary port completes. You place all future construction, collect weekly income, and keep the title permanently.
Construction PointsThe "currency" of expansion. Tier-1 builds generate points; Tier-2 and Tier-3 builds spend them. See Section 06.
Economy InfluenceEach facility nudges the system toward an economy type (Extraction, Industrial, High-Tech, Agriculture, Military, Tourism, Refinery, Colony). The mix sets what the markets sell and buy.
05

How To Get Started

The end-to-end procedure for your first colony. Read it all once before you claim anything — the early choices (which faction you buy from, which system you pick) are locked in and shape everything after.

  1. Scout a target system first

    Open the Galaxy Map and look for an unpopulated system within roughly 15 light-years of existing populated space. (This range started at 10 ly in early beta and has been tuned upward; treat 15 ly as the working figure and confirm in-game.) Visit it and honk/scan it. In the system map you can see the preset primary-port location (a circled flag) and the count of available orbital and surface build slots before committing.

  2. Choose where you buy the claim — this sets your faction

    Buy the claim at a Colonisation Contact in a station whose controlling minor faction is the one you want to found your colony. That faction is carried into your new system as its native, permanent controlling faction — and it sets your colony's superpower allegiance: Federation, Empire or Alliance (see Section 10).

  3. Pick your primary port type & pay the claim

    You select the initial port: an Outpost (cheapest, medium pad, ~19k commodities), a Coriolis (large pad, ~54k), or a large Orbis/Ocellus (~209k). The registration fee is paid in credits — budget on the order of ~25 million CR; it varies with the port chosen. The claim then locks the system to you for 24 hours.

  4. Fly out and drop the beacon (within 24h)

    Travel to the claimed system, fly to the flagged location, and launch the Colonisation Beacon via the Colonisation Suite. Miss the 24-hour window and the claim is revoked. Once dropped, the Colonisation Ship arrives — note the primary site can be hundreds of light-seconds from the arrival star, so factor in supercruise time per round trip.

  5. Haul the commodity list to the Colonisation Ship

    Dock with the CS and open its construction panel to see the required commodities and live progress. Buy these from nearby populated markets and ferry them in. You have a hard deadline — about 28 days (≈4 weeks) — to deliver the entire primary-port list, or all progress is lost. Plan to begin only when you have the full window available.

  6. Wait for the weekly tick

    When the last commodity is delivered, the port appears in a partially-built, services-offline state. It finalises at the next server maintenance tick (Thursday ~07:00 UTC). The Colonisation Ship departs, the beacon becomes a permanent nav beacon, and you are now the System Architect.

  7. Expand at your own pace

    A Colonisation Contact now lives in your finished port. From the system map (orbital) or by flying to a surface site (planetary), you place new construction. Orbital ports carry their own ~28-day build timers; many surface settlements have no timer — handy for slow, chill building between other activities.

Intel — Changing Your Mind

You can cancel a claim from any Colonisation Contact and re-claim — even from a different faction's station — if you picked the wrong founding faction or system. You forfeit the original ~25M fee and any commodities already delivered, and you may have to wait for the old Colonisation Ship to leave before re-deploying. Cheaper to get it right the first time.

06

Tiers & Construction Points

After the primary port, every build is gated by a points economy. This is the part new architects find confusing, so here it is plainly.

The three tiers

Tier 1 · free

T1 — earns points

Outposts, basic installations, small/medium surface ports and settlements. They cost no construction points and each one awards a Tier-2 point.

Tier 2 · spends T2

T2 — the middle

Coriolis starports, Asteroid Bases, hubs, and most "Station"-class installations. They spend T2 points and award T3 points.

Tier 3 · spends T3

T3 — the showpieces

The big stuff — Ocellus, Orbis, large surface ports, and the Dodec. They spend T3 points and award nothing further.

The points loop in one line

Build free T1 facilities → bank T2 points → spend them on T2 facilities → bank T3 points → spend them on the T3 showpieces. Each tier funds the next.

Costs that escalate

Base costs are 3 points for a T2 port and 6 points for a T3 port. Frontier confirmed (and later documented) that these costs rise as you build more big ports in one system: from the moment your second Tier-2/Tier-3 port begins construction, costs climb — T2 goes 3 → 5 → 7, and T3 goes 6 → 12 → 18 — for each subsequent large port. This is intentional, to make you build out the supporting economy rather than spam mega-stations.

Efficiency Tip

Large surface settlements are the best point source. A large Tier-2 settlement awards 2× Tier-3 points for a modest commodity cost (~8,500 t). Stacking a few of these is the standard way to bankroll an Ocellus, Orbis or Dodec.

The "go big first" shortcut

The first facility you place in a brand-new system never costs points — so you can make a Tier-3 station your primary port and skip the entire points climb. It's wildly resource-intensive (200k+ tonnes) and usually needs a squadron, but it's the move for a system you intend as a true capital with few build slots.

Warning — Build Slots Are Finite

Every system has a fixed number of orbital and surface slots, shown in the architect view. A system with only ~4 slots may never generate enough points to add a T3 station later — so if you want a large station there, build it primary, or pick a richer system. Check slot counts before you claim.

07

Economy: Shaping Your System

This is where colonisation becomes design. The facilities you place determine the system's economy, which determines what its markets trade, what missions appear, and what the system is good for.

How economy is set

  • A fresh system with only a generic primary port shows the Colony economy — a placeholder "no specialism yet" state with a limited market.
  • Each facility carries an economy influence (Extraction, Industrial, High-Tech, Agriculture, Military, Tourism, Refinery, Contraband). Build mining facilities → the system trends Extraction; build farms → Agriculture; and so on.
  • Planetary body type matters. The body a port orbits or sits on contributes a base economy — e.g. a rocky body gives a Refinery lean, which (with the right surface port) is how systems produce CMM Composite, the notorious bottleneck commodity.

The August 2025 simplification

Early colonisation economy maths was brutal — economies "cannibalised" each other and it was easy to ruin a market by mixing types. On 21 August 2025 Frontier greatly simplified it: in any given market, the top two economies are each treated as if no other economy were present. In plain terms — you can now run a couple of strong economies side by side without them dragging each other down, so a mixed Extraction + Industrial system actually works. (Older community economy guides written before this date can mislead you; weight anything pre-Aug-2025 accordingly.)

Intel — Economy → Profession Map

Extraction = mining markets, mineral demand. Industrial = manufacturing, machinery, good for trade. High-Tech = advanced goods, research missions, often best credit margins. Agriculture = food supply, feeds other economies. Military = security, weapons, combat-flavoured missions. Tourism = passenger & sightseeing demand. Refinery = produces CMM Composite & refined metals. Colony = generalist starter.

09

Logistics & The Haul

Colonisation lives or dies on hauling efficiency. A Coriolis primary is ~69 trips in a big hauler; a T3 station is ~270+. Minimise distance and maximise cargo.

Fleet for the job

  • A dedicated bulk hauler — the Type-9 Heavy — is the natural workhorse: large cargo hold and a large pad, no rank required.
  • An Imperial Cutter is the gold-standard hauler if you hold the Imperial rank for one (huge cargo, fast shields); a Type-9 does the job without it.
  • A Fleet Carrier is the force-multiplier: park it beside the source market or at the build site, pre-load its 25,000 t hold with commodities bought in bulk, then shuttle short hops. Strongly worth it for any T2+ build.

Sourcing the commodities

The big-ticket items are Steel, CMM Composite, Aluminium, Titanium, and Ceramic/Liquid materials. CMM Composite is the classic bottleneck — buy it wherever Refinery/Industrial economies sell it, and consider building a Refinery-leaning surface port in your own system later so the colony produces its own. Use a market finder (INARA / EDDB-style tools) to locate high-supply, low-price sources close to your build site.

Time-savers worth learning

  • Supercruise Assist drop trick: with SCA fitted, push to 100% throttle at the right distance and let it yank you out at the pad — shaves several seconds off every single approach, which adds up over 200 trips.
  • Heatsink + fuel-scoop spool: pop a heatsink while scooping to spool the FSD and cut multi-jump times.
  • Carrier ship-swap loop: keep two haulers in your carrier's shipyard right by the build site; load one, return, abandon-ship to respawn, swap to the second while the first transfers — halves landing overhead at awkward surface stations.
  • Group up: any Commander can deliver to your construction site. Split commodity types across squad-mates and the haul collapses dramatically.
Tool of the Trade

EDColony and DaftMav's colonisation construction sheets track your active depot, total what's still needed, and compute remaining trips for your specific ship. Run one of these from trip one — flying blind on a 270-trip build is how people burn out. Links in Section 12.

10

Superpower Alignment

Your colony's permanent controlling faction is inherited from the station where you buy the claim — and that single choice decides which superpower your system answers to: its missions, the ship line you can buy at your own shipyard, and whose footprint you extend. Pick it deliberately; it can't be changed without re-claiming.

How alignment is set

  1. Choose the founding station. Find a populated station whose controlling minor faction holds the allegiance you want — Federation, Empire, or Alliance.
  2. Buy the claim there. That minor faction is carried into your new system as its native, permanent controlling faction — and it stays, even through later Powerplay shifts.
  3. Inherit the allegiance. Your colony now flies that flag: aligned missions, that superpower's ship line at your shipyard once you build the right ports, and a base from which to push its influence.

A secondary faction slot can be seeded by your squadron's aligned faction, if you fly in one. Independent NPC factions may also migrate in over time via the BGS.

Federation

A Federal founding faction makes the system Federal: Federal-flavoured missions, the option to push a Federal Power's influence, and — once you build the right ports — the Federal ship line (Dropship, Assault Ship, Gunship, Corvette) at your own shipyard. Those ships still require Federal Navy rank to buy and fly; a colony you control doesn't hand you rank, but a healthy founding faction is a reliable, controllable mission source to grind it on your own turf.

Empire

An Imperial founding faction makes the system Imperial: Imperial missions, the option to extend an Imperial Power, and the Imperial ship line (Courier, Clipper, Cutter) at your shipyard once the ports support it — again gated by Imperial Navy rank. The benefit mirrors the Federal case: a colony is a rank-grind engine you own, not a rank shortcut.

Alliance

An Alliance founding faction makes the system Alliance-aligned. The Alliance runs no Navy and no rank ladder, so there's no rank tailwind to chase — but Alliance ships need no rank either, so nothing is gated behind one. A colony is most useful here as BGS and Powerplay leverage: it plants or strengthens an Alliance presence in a system you shaped.

Important Distinction

Local minor-faction allegiance and Powerplay are separate systems. Your founding faction's allegiance makes the colony Federal, Imperial or Alliance for BGS and mission purposes; whether a Power controls the system in Powerplay is a different contest. Don't expect colonising to move your Powerplay standing on its own.

Commander Note

Alignment is the one early decision you can't cheaply undo — it's locked the moment you buy the claim. Before you pay, be sure the station's controlling faction matches the superpower whose ships and missions you actually want, and ideally sits close to your build site so the haul stays short.

Your Colony Blueprint

A concrete first-colony plan. The shape is the same whatever your priority — pick the economy focus and superpower allegiance that match how you play, then follow the order below.

System selection criteria (scout for all of these)

  • At least one ring-bearing planet — ideally metallic/metal-rich rings. Rings give you RES sites (for mining and combat bounties) once live, and let you build an Asteroid Base.
  • A landable rocky body — for surface settlements (your point engine) and a Refinery-lean port to self-produce CMM Composite.
  • Generous build-slot count (check the architect view) — room for your chosen economy plus its supporting economies, so avoid slot-poor 4-slot systems.
  • Something scenic if convenient (notable bodies / nearby nebula) — feeds a later Tourism layer for passenger work.
  • Close to a source market of your chosen allegiance — keeps the founding faction the one you want and the haul short.

Suggested build order

  1. Primary: Coriolis. A large pad (so big ships dock and you can sell/rearm) at a manageable ~54k-tonne / ~69-trip cost. Buy it from a station of your chosen allegiance so the system is aligned from birth. A cheaper Outpost is fine for a faster first win, but the medium pad limits you.
  2. Stack large surface settlements for points. Drop 2–3 large surface settlements in the economies you want — each banks 2× T3 points while setting your economy mix.
  3. Build your primary economy spine. Run the settlement → installation → Hub chain for your focus (Military Hub for security/combat, Extraction Hub for mining, High-Tech or Refinery Hub for trade). The Hub is what pushes the system decisively toward that economy.
  4. Add a second economy. An Asteroid Base on your best ring (Extraction), or an Industrial/High-Tech port — viable now that the top-two economies don't cannibalise each other.
  5. Layer Tourism + Refinery later. A Satellite → Tourism settlement/installation opens passenger demand; a Refinery-lean surface port lets the colony make its own CMM Composite, easing all future builds. These are the "chill" no-timer surface builds.
  6. Capstone: a T3 station when points allow. Cash banked T3 points into an Ocellus, Orbis, or Dodec for the population/wealth/tech spike. By now your own Refinery output and Extraction markets shorten the haul.

Pitfalls & Gotchas

  • The 28-day primary clock is real. Don't claim before you can commit the time/cargo. Missing it loses everything delivered.
  • Primary ports can't be demolished. Choose the type and location deliberately — it's permanent.
  • Escalating point costs punish station-spam. Build supporting facilities (especially large settlements) before reaching for multiple big ports.
  • Few build slots = no late T3. If you want a mega-station and the system is slot-poor, build it primary or pick a different system.
  • Founding faction is locked at claim. Buy from a station of the allegiance you actually want; fixing it later means forfeiting the fee and re-hauling.
  • Old guides predate the August 2025 economy rework. Economy advice written earlier may steer you wrong about mixing economies — they no longer cannibalise the top two.
  • Surface ports/settlements can be far apart and far from the star. Build-site distance is a real time cost — scout the layout before committing.
  • Visual bugs exist. A finished facility may render as a different model than you chose; the underlying data is what counts.

Pre-Flight Checklist

  1. Target system

    Identified a target system within ~15 ly: rings present, landable rocky body, plenty of build slots, near a market of your chosen allegiance.

  2. Allegiance

    Confirmed the claim station's controlling faction holds the superpower allegiance you want.

  3. Port & budget

    Decided primary port type (Coriolis recommended for the large pad) and budgeted ~25M CR.

  4. Window & hauler

    Cleared a 28-day window and prepped a bulk hauler (and Fleet Carrier, if available).

  5. Material sources

    Located bulk sources for Steel, CMM Composite, Aluminium, Titanium close to the build site.

  6. Construction tracker

    Set up EDColony / a construction tracker before the first delivery.

  7. Build-order plan

    Plan banked: large surface settlements for points → your economy's Hub spine → a second economy → Tourism/Refinery later → T3 capstone.

  8. Log it

    Logged the new colony in your Fleet Tracker once it goes live and the Architect title lands.

Claim deliberately, haul efficiently, build the economy before the showpiece — and you'll have a permanent system, aligned how you chose, with your name on it. Fly safe, Architect.
