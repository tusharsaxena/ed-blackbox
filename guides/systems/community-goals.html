Section 00 — Orientation

00Briefing

This manual assumes you have never participated in a Community Goal (CG) and explains the system from first principles: what it is, why it exists, what you gain, and the precise sequence of actions to take part. It then layers in optimization, a dedicated combat deep-dive (your primary playstyle), and a Federation-alignment playbook aimed at your eventual Federal Corvette.

A CG is the single most beginner-friendly, high-yield activity in Elite Dangerous precisely because the game does the hard part — coordinating thousands of pilots — for you. You just turn up and do what you already do (shoot, haul, ferry, mine), then collect a payout that scales with the whole community's effort. Your existing fleet already covers every CG category that exists.

Read this first

CGs are opt-in. Nothing you do counts toward a CG until you have flown to the host system and signed up at the mission board. The most common rookie mistake is grinding bounties or cargo for hours, then discovering none of it registered because the sign-up step was skipped. Sign up first.

Section 01 — Definition

01What a Community Goal Is

A Community Goal is a time-limited, server-wide cooperative event where every commander in the galaxy can contribute toward one shared objective sponsored by an in-fiction faction or megacorporation. They were the headline feature of game update 1.1 back in 2015 and have run continuously ever since — typically one to two active at any time, usually starting on a Thursday and running for roughly a week (occasionally up to four weeks, or ending early if the community blows through every target).

Mechanically, a CG is a giant shared mission. The sponsoring faction asks the playerbase to deliver something — combat bounty vouchers, combat bonds, trade commodities, mined ore, ferried passengers, exploration or exobiology data — toward a single galaxy-scale counter. Crucially, a CG is simultaneously cooperative and competitive: everyone pushes the same counter up together, but you are also individually ranked against every other participant for a share of the reward pool.

The fiction is not decoration. CG outcomes genuinely reshape the lived galaxy. Past goals have built brand-new starports, megaships and surface settlements, upgraded outposts into full starports, expanded station services, and decided the victor of system-level conflicts. The very first CG ever run constructed the Orbis starport Unity in the New Yembo system — a structure still in the galaxy today.

All play modes count

CGs are powered by the shared Background Simulation, so they progress identically whether you play in Open, Private Group, or Solo. You can even switch modes mid-goal. You never have to expose yourself to PvP to participate — a major reassurance for a primarily PvE commander.

Section 02 — The Payoff

02Why They Matter

CGs are widely regarded as the most legitimate "get-rich" loop in the game, but the credits are only one of five reasons to run them.

▸ Reward 01
Credits

Payouts scale with the global completion tier reached and your individual contribution percentile. Top brackets routinely pay tens of millions for a few evenings of work.

▸ Reward 02
Exclusive Unlocks

Many CGs grant rewards money can't otherwise buy: limited-availability or pre-engineered modules, permits, discounts on hulls/outfitting, decals, paint jobs and bobbleheads.

▸ Reward 03
Reputation

Contributing builds reputation with the sponsoring minor faction, which flows up to its superpower — the engine behind Federal & Imperial rank.

▸ Reward 04
Galaxy Impact

Your tonnage and kills literally help build the station or win the war. CGs are the clearest way a solo pilot leaves a permanent mark on the map.

▸ Reward 05
Throughput

A CG concentrates demand in one place: a single station buying one commodity at inflated prices, or one RES worth of targets all paying toward your counter. Less searching, more doing.

For your profile specifically: CGs let you do the combat you already enjoy, get paid combat-grade money and stack Federation reputation toward the Corvette — three birds, one trigger pull.

Section 03 — Audience

03Who They're For

Everyone, at every stage. A brand-new commander in a stock Sidewinder can meaningfully contribute to a bounty-hunting CG and bank a six-figure reward; a fully-engineered fleet owner can dominate a Top-10 leaderboard. Because rewards scale relative to the community, you are never priced out — you simply earn a bracket appropriate to the effort you put in.

CGs are an especially good fit if you:

  • Want structured objectives rather than open-ended sandbox grinding — the game hands you a destination and a task.
  • Enjoy a specific role (combat, hauling, ferrying, mining) and want it to pay better than usual.
  • Are working toward superpower rank and want reputation gains bundled with activity you'd do anyway.
  • Like the idea of your individual play moving a galaxy-wide needle alongside thousands of others.

Your established role-specialized fleet — combat flagship, dedicated hauler, dedicated miner, passenger liner, multirole — means you are already equipped for any CG type that appears. Most commanders have to scramble to fit a ship; you just pick the right hull from the hangar.

Section 04 — Mechanics

04Anatomy of a Community Goal

Every CG is scored on two independent axes. Understanding both is the whole game.

Axis 1 — The Global Completion Tier

The community as a whole fills a progress bar through a series of tiers, usually Tier 1 through Tier 10 (Frontier sometimes extends to Tier 11 if T10 falls early, or pulls the ceiling down to end a goal on schedule). Each tier the playerbase unlocks raises the size of the entire reward pool. A CG that only reaches T3 pays everyone far less than the same CG completed to T10. This is the cooperative half: every commander's contribution helps drag the whole bar upward, benefiting all participants.

Axis 2 — Your Individual Percentile Bracket

Within that pool, your slice is determined by where your personal contribution ranks against every other participant. The brackets are fixed percentile bands — your reward jumps each time you climb into a higher one:

Top 100%Participation floor
Top 75%Light effort
Top 50%Solid evening
Top 25%Committed run
Top 10%Serious grind
Top 10 CMDRsLeaderboard / prestige

You are paid the minimum pool figure at Top 100% and the maximum at Top 10%, with the Top 10 Commanders receiving the single largest payout — a prestige tier worth chasing if you commit hard to one goal.

Your bracket can slip

Brackets are relative, not banked. If you stop contributing while others keep going, you can be overtaken and slide from, say, Top 25% down to Top 50% by the time the goal closes. This is why the final hours of a CG matter most — heavy contributors dump everything at the end to lock their position. Don't assume an early lead is safe.

Claiming Your Reward

Rewards are not paid instantly. When the CG ends, you collect at the host station, ideally within two weeks. Earned credits auto-deposit to your balance if you forget; other rewards (modules, decals, etc.) remain available for pickup at the host station essentially indefinitely. There is no penalty for collecting late — but get the special modules before you forget which goal they came from.

Combat CGs (your bread and butter)

These come in two distinct flavours that are easy to confuse — and the distinction is one you already track carefully:

  • Bounty-hunting CGs ask for bounty vouchers: kill wanted ships (at Resource Extraction Sites, Nav Beacons, etc.) and hand the vouchers in. This is the classic "telethon" combat CG.
  • Conflict-Zone CGs ask for combat bonds: pick a side in a Conflict Zone and destroy the enemy faction's ships. Bonds and bounties are separate currencies — make sure you're earning the one the CG actually wants.

Both types reward assisted kills (you only need to land meaningful damage before the kill registers), which makes RES "tag-and-move" tactics viable even in a modest ship.

PowerPlay CGs — note for a future Federalist

A newer breed ties CGs directly into PowerPlay campaigns — recent examples include the dueling "Opening Federal Campaign" and "Opening Independence Campaign" for the same contested system. These can advance Federation interests directly, but they interact with PowerPlay pledging and merits. Since you are currently working Imperial rank and sit Hostile to a specific Imperial Power, read the goal's faction and Power alignment carefully before committing — see Section 09.

Section 06 — Operations Sequence

06Getting Started: Step by Step

This is the full loop, from "I've never done one" to "reward in the bank." Do it once and it's second nature.

  1. Find the active goals

    Check what's running before you fly anywhere. Three reliable sources: the in-game Galaxy Map (active CG systems show a distinctive gold marker), the official Elite Dangerous Community Goals page, and — best of all — the INARA Community Goals tracker, which shows live global progress, your own contribution, and estimated bracket boundaries.

  2. Read the brief

    Each CG has a GalNet write-up stating the exact deliverable, the sponsoring faction, the host station, and any constraints (e.g. "bounties for Faction X"). Decide whether the activity, location, and reward justify the trip. A 20,000 Ly goal paying a few million is rarely worth it; a goal a few jumps from the Bubble usually is.

  3. Sign up — from anywhere

    You can register from any station's Mission Board → Community Goals tab without flying there first. Open the goal and select Sign Up. This is mandatory and the most-skipped step. Until you sign up, nothing counts.

  4. Pick the right hull

    Match the ship to the deliverable using the table in Section 05. Combat → Python Mk II. Trade → Type-9. Passengers → Lynx Highliner. Mining → Type-11. Fit a cargo rack if it's a trade/mining CG and confirm the right module set before you leave Ray Gateway.

  5. Travel to the host system

    Signing up is remote, but contributing is local. You must physically be in the CG system, earning/delivering for the sponsoring faction, for it to register. For combat goals, the qualifying RES / Conflict Zone is in or immediately around the host system.

  6. Do the work & bank often

    Earn the deliverable, then hand it in at the host station's relevant Contact (bounty/bond voucher office, Commodities market, passenger lounge, etc.). Bank frequently — uncashed bounty vouchers are lost if your ship is destroyed before you redeem them. Track progress live on the Transactions tab of your left HUD panel.

  7. Monitor your bracket

    Watch INARA and the in-game CG screen to see which percentile you're in and how far the next bracket is. Decide whether "one more run" is worth it (Section 07). Remember your standing can erode as others contribute.

  8. Collect your reward

    When the goal ends, return to the host station and claim at the CG board / Contacts. Grab any special modules or cosmetics in person; credits will auto-deposit if you don't.

Section 07 — Optimization

07Strategy & Optimization

The "one more run" calculation

The core decision in every CG is whether the next batch of effort crosses a bracket boundary. Because bracket jumps are worth far more than linear contribution, knowing you're 20 tonnes / one bounty stack short of Top 25% changes everything. INARA and dedicated CG threshold calculators estimate these boundaries from community-submitted data and tell you how many more runs a bracket costs. Use them; don't grind blind.

Time the endgame

Brackets are relative and most heavily contested in the final 24 hours, when committed players dump their stockpiles. Two viable approaches: contribute steadily and accept some bracket drift, or — if chasing Top 10% / Top 10 — hold a reserve and push hard near the close to leapfrog people who finished early.

Stack and double-dip

On combat CGs you can run a kill-mission for the same faction and earn the CG vouchers from the same kills — collecting mission reward and CG credit simultaneously. On trade CGs, source the commodity in bulk where it's cheapest and confirm the CG station's buy price; the inflated CG demand often makes the haul profitable on its own before the reward even lands.

Juggle concurrent goals

When two CGs of the same type run at once (or two phases of one campaign), check whether one shares a system or commodity with the other. Stacking compatible goals doubles your reward for the same activity. When they conflict (opposing PowerPlay campaigns), pick the side that matches your long-term allegiance.

Mode flexibility is a tool

If Open feels risky near a popular combat CG (gankers do camp host stations), drop to Solo or a Private Group. Progress and rewards are identical. There is no mechanical reason to take PvP risk for a CG unless you want to.

Section 08 — Primary Doctrine

08Combat CG Deep-Dive

Since your activity is heavily skewed to PvE combat, this is the section that matters most. Combat CGs are also the most consistently available — there is almost always one running.

Bounty-hunting CG workflow

  1. Sign up, fly to the CG system, locate a HIGH or LOW Resource Extraction Site (avoid Hazardous unless you want the heat — they're unpoliced).
  2. Fit and deploy a Kill Warrant Scanner. It raises bounty values and, critically, lets you reliably accrue vouchers for the faction the CG actually wants in multi-faction systems.
  3. Follow the system security ships (green contacts) and engage whatever they're attacking. Tag wanted targets — assisted kills pay you the full bounty, so even chipping in damage before the police finish a kill counts.
  4. Bank to the CG station before your hold of vouchers gets dangerous. Repeat.

Conflict-Zone CG workflow

  1. Confirm the CG wants combat bonds, not bounties — they are different currencies handed in at different contacts.
  2. Drop into a Conflict Zone in/near the CG system and, in the side-selection tab, choose the faction the CG is sponsoring.
  3. Destroy the opposing faction's ships for bonds; redeem them at the combat-bond contact, which credits the CG.
Ship note — Python Mk II

Your medium combat flagship is ideal here. Its 6 optional internals force trade-offs (HRPs, MRPs, SCBs and the shield generator all compete for the same bays), so build for sustained RES/CZ uptime rather than a glass-cannon burst: bi-weave with Resistance Augmented, Heavy-Duty boosters/HRPs, and at least one SCB to ride out long sessions without docking. For lighter or assist-tagging duty, the Viper Mk IV gets you in and out fast and cheap.

Don't lose your vouchers

Bounty vouchers held in your hold are forfeit if you're destroyed before redeeming. In a busy combat CG, bank in shorter cycles than you would for normal bounty hunting. (Combat bonds are credited on kill and are safer, but the discipline of frequent banking is good practice either way.)

Section 09 — Allegiance Strategy

09Federation Alignment Playbook

You want to drift toward the Federation for an eventual Federal Corvette while you're currently grinding Imperial rank. CGs can serve both ends, but the interactions need care.

How CG rewards build toward the Corvette

Reputation in Elite Dangerous is unified: doing work for a minor faction raises your standing with that faction and with its parent superpower. Complete a CG sponsored by a Federation-aligned faction and you bank Federation reputation alongside the credits. Reputation is what fills your Federal Navy rank bar — and the Corvette requires Rear Admiral, the second-highest Federal rank, plus a hull cost around 187M CR. CGs are therefore a way to make Corvette-fund credits and Corvette-unlock rank from the same activity.

Rank vs. reputation — the crucial distinction

Reputation fills the rank bar, but it does not promote you. Each Federal rank-up requires completing a specific "Federal Navy" promotion mission that appears only once the bar is full and only from factions you're allied with. CGs accelerate the bar; you still hand-finish each rank with its named Navy mission. (This mirrors the Imperial Navy mechanic you already know.)

You can serve two masters

There is no penalty for being allied with both the Federation and the Empire at once — superpower reputations are tracked independently and don't cancel each other. You can finish your Imperial Squire/Achenar-permit grind and climb the Federal ladder in parallel. The only real friction is reputation decay: standing above 75% with a superpower slowly bleeds back toward 75% over time, so the way to stay Allied is to keep feeding it — periodic Federation CGs are a tidy way to top that up.

PowerPlay caution — read the faction first

PowerPlay CGs are not automatically aligned to your interests. You are currently Hostile to a specific Imperial Power (Lavigny-Duval) and pledged into the PowerPlay layer. Before joining any PowerPlay CG, confirm two things: (1) the sponsoring faction's superpower allegiance, and (2) which Power the campaign serves. A Federation-allegiance PowerPlay CG advances your Corvette goal; an Imperial one may undercut your current Imperial rank grind or your PowerPlay standing. When in doubt, stick to plain (non-PowerPlay) Federation-faction CGs — they give clean Federal reputation without the PowerPlay entanglement.

Practical Federalist CG routine

  • Favour combat/trade/passenger CGs whose host faction is Federation-aligned — check allegiance on INARA before flying.
  • Bank reputation opportunistically: even a Top 50% finish on a Fed CG nudges the Federal bar while paying well.
  • Keep your Imperial grind separate: run Imperial-faction donation/courier missions (Cemiess) for the Achenar permit, and Federation CGs for the Corvette track — they don't conflict.
  • Top up before decay bites: once you cross into Allied with the Federation, a single periodic Fed CG keeps you there.
Section 10 — Failure Modes

10Pitfalls & Gotchas

✕ Trap 01
Forgetting to sign up

Hours of work register as zero. Always sign up at the Mission Board before you start earning.

✕ Trap 02
Wrong currency

Earning combat bonds for a bounty CG (or vice-versa). Confirm the exact deliverable in the brief.

✕ Trap 03
Wrong faction

Bounties/bonds must be for the CG's sponsoring faction. A Kill Warrant Scanner keeps your vouchers on-target.

✕ Trap 04
Dying with vouchers

Unbanked bounty vouchers are lost on destruction. Bank in short cycles during combat CGs.

✕ Trap 05
Bracket complacency

An early Top 25% can slide to Top 50% if you stop. The last day is where positions are decided.

✕ Trap 06
Chasing distant CGs

A goal thousands of light-years out rarely pays for the travel time. Weigh reward against distance.

✕ Trap 07
Local-only delivery

You can sign up anywhere, but you must earn/deliver in the host system. Vouchers from elsewhere may not count.

✕ Trap 08
Blind PowerPlay joins

A PowerPlay CG may serve a Power you oppose. Check allegiance before committing.
