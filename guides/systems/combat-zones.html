01

What a Combat Zone Is

A temporary battle instance, born from a war between two minor factions.

A Combat Zone (CZ) is a transient signal source that appears only in systems where two minor factions are in a War or Civil War state. It is not a permanent feature of a system — when the war ends, the zones vanish. This is the single most important framing fact: a CZ is always tied to two specific factions fighting each other, and everything you do inside it pushes one of them toward winning.

There are two completely separate kinds, with separate rules, separate rewards, and separate skill demands:

  • Ship CZs — fought in your ship, in space. Drop in from supercruise, pick a side, destroy enemy ships.
  • On-foot CZs — fought on foot at a planetary settlement, infantry-style, accessed through Frontline Solutions. Covered fully in §11.

Ships destroyed in a CZ do not drop materials — so this is not a place to farm engineering mats. It is a place to make credits, build reputation, swing a war, and rank up combat.

// Core loop

Find a system at war → drop into a zone → choose the side opposing your targets → destroy enemy ships (each = a bond + a tick on the score) → optionally clear the special objectives → leave before you die → redeem bonds at a station. Repeat.

02

Finding Zones & Reading Intensity

No war, no zone. Higher intensity, tougher ships, bigger payouts.

Locating a war

Because CZs only exist during active wars, the hunt is really a hunt for a system in War / Civil War. Two reliable approaches:

  • In-game: jump into a candidate system. If a war is active, conflict zones show up in the Navigation panel as signal sources. Supercruise toward one and drop in.
  • Out-of-game: use Inara to search a faction or system and read its current states (War / Civil War), or edtools.cc/pve to find active combat systems. States rotate constantly, so check fresh.

Reading intensity — it's hidden in your ship

The little + marks next to a CZ signal in the nav panel are not the intensity rating. To learn a zone's tier before committing:

  • Open the System Map → planetary map → select the CZ; it shows a threat number.
  • Or dock and talk to Frontline Solutions, which lists each zone as Low / Medium / High intensity.
IntensityEnemy shipsSpecial spawnsRough payout (full zone)
LowMostly stock; the odd Elite-ranked pilot, no engineeringNo Spec Ops wings~500k CR
MediumLightly engineered, mixed ranksSpec Ops wings, Captains, correspondents/propagandistslow single-digit millions
HighHeavily engineered, high-rank pilotsAll of the above, plus a chance of a Capital Ship if the Empire or Federation is a combatant~5–20M CR
// Difficulty is not linear

Even Low zones can contain Elite-rank NPCs, and ships are combat-fitted — tougher than the random traffic you meet in the wild. Climb the ladder deliberately: Low → Medium → High. Don't take a glass cannon into a High expecting the rep to carry you.

// Anti-bully mechanic

Bringing an over-engineered monster to a Low or Medium will summon hefty enemy Spec Ops wings to even the odds. The zone scales to punish stomping, so match the ship to the tier rather than over-gunning a soft zone.

03

Entering & Choosing a Side

The one action that makes everything else count — do it before you fire.

On dropping in, a prompt appears in the Comms panel (message tab) asking which faction you want to fight for. This is mandatory:

  1. Open the Comms panel and select a side.
  2. Your radar instantly repaints: green = allies, red = hostiles. The red ships are now your legal targets and will engage you.
  3. Only now do you open fire.
// Fire before you side and everyone turns on you

If you shoot a single shot before choosing a faction, the entire zone — both sides — treats you as hostile. Bonds won't accrue and you'll be swarmed. Always side first.

Two edge cases

  • "Ceasefire" instead of a side menu: the war has ended. The zone is a husk, kills won't register, and any mission tied to it is dead. Leave; mission clears at the next daily server tick.
  • Purple contacts (in a wing): a wingmate fired on an ally. Whoever does not have a purple target should leave and re-drop, or the zone may flip everyone hostile. Solo, you won't see this.
// Picking the RIGHT side for missions

You destroy the side you did not join. For a "Kill Faction X Ships" mission, join X's enemy so that X's ships become the red targets. Side with X by mistake and you'll be killing their opponent — zero mission progress.

04

Scoring & "Kills Confirmed"

The score that wins the zone is separate from the bonds in your wallet.

Each side has a victory bar shown top-right (the HUD readout at the top of this dossier). Every enemy you destroy adds to your side's score and is relayed in your comms feed — destroyed enemies, lost allies, and warnings as either side nears victory or defeat. Fill your bar before the enemy fills theirs and your side wins the zone.

Crucially, two things happen per kill, and they are independent:

  • The victory tally — pushes your side toward winning the engagement (BGS effect, see §9). Winning is not required to keep your bonds or your mission kills.
  • A combat bond — the credit voucher that goes in your wallet (see §6).
// Why your mission counter sometimes lags the kills

Mission progress tracks qualifying kills independently of the victory bar. So a kill can pay a bond and move the score yet not tick a mission — usually because the dead ship wasn't the mission's target faction, or because of how stacked missions count (see §8). The zone score, the bonds, and each mission's tally are three separate ledgers.

05

Special Objectives

Optional, but they swing the score hard and pay well. None are required to win.

From Medium intensity up, scripted events spawn into the zone. Killing the hostile versions (or protecting the friendly ones) gives bonds plus a big jolt to your side's victory tally.

High-value kill

Captain

A large engineered ship — typically Anaconda, Corvette or Cutter — flown by an Elite pilot. Enemy: hunt it down and kill it. Friendly: keep it alive until it jumps out.

Tip: target the FSD or powerplant to drop it fast.

Elite wing

Spec Ops

Four heavily engineered medium ships with unique effects. If hostile, kill all four fast — they can wipe your allies and lose you the fight. If friendly, leave them be.

Warning: these do NOT count toward combat-bond / massacre missions.

Escort

War Correspondent

A non-combat ship that must be protected if it's on your side. Keep hostiles off it until it departs.

Priority target

Propagandist

An enemy ship that must be destroyed. Prioritise it when it appears.

High intensity only

Capital Ship

Appears only in High zones when the Empire or Federation is a combatant. It cannot be destroyed — disable it by targeting and destroying its heat relays (cycle sub-targets) to force it to flee.

// Spec Ops vs Capital — usually one or the other

In a High zone, if Spec Ops spawns the capital ship typically won't, and vice versa. Neither is mandatory for victory; they're accelerators and paydays, not win conditions.

06

Combat Bonds & Making Credits

Vouchers, not instant cash — and they evaporate if you die.

Every enemy you destroy after siding awards a combat bond voucher. Value is fixed per ship type — heavier ships pay more — and ranges from a few thousand up to roughly 80,000 CR for the biggest hulls. (Destroy a ship without having chosen a side and it's a flat ~3,000 CR, another reason to side first.)

Income expectations

ZonePer-ship bondRealistic full-zone haul
Lowsmall~500k CR
Mediummoderatea few million
Highup to ~80k each5–20M CR (skill/ship dependent)

Target priority for income

  • Prioritise the big hulls — Corvettes, Cutters, Anacondas, Pythons — for the fattest bonds.
  • If none are in range, use "Select Highest Threat" targeting. It finds your next kill and tends to put allied NPCs on the same target, giving you a free wingman against tough ships.

Redeeming & protecting your bonds

  • Bonds are redeemed at the Combat Bond Contact in a station's Contacts menu, at a station in the system controlled by the faction you fought for.
  • They behave like bounty vouchers: unredeemed bonds are lost if your ship is destroyed. Bank often — when a fight turns against you, jump out and cash in before re-engaging.
// The rebuy maths

A High zone can pay 10M+, but a single death there forfeits every unbanked bond and charges your rebuy. Net result of a greedy wipe can be deeply negative. Treat "leave to bank" as a core part of the loop, not an interruption to it.

07

Superpower Reputation & Rank (Empire)

CZs do feed Empire rep — but they are not the rank-up itself.

This is the section you actually care about for the Achenar / engineer grind, so here's the honest mechanic, layered:

  1. Winning a zone raises your reputation with the faction you fought for and lowers it with the side you destroyed.
  2. Redeeming combat bonds for an Empire-aligned faction raises your standing with that faction — and anything that raises reputation with an Empire-aligned faction nudges your Imperial rank bar upward.
  3. For the rep/rank to land on Empire, both ends must line up: fight for an Imperial-aligned minor faction and cash the bonds at an Imperial station. Cash Imperial-earned bonds at a Federal station and the superpower credit can skew the wrong way.
// The rank bar still needs a promotion mission

CZ rep can fill the Imperial rank bar, but reputation and naval rank are separate systems. Once the bar hits 100%, you still must find and complete an "Imperial Navy"-titled promotion mission (board-flip to roll one). No amount of CZ grinding promotes you on its own.

// CMDR Ka0s — read this before flying out

For your Squire → Achenar goal, CZs are a bonus source of Empire rep, not the efficient path. The rank-bar gain from combat is small per action, and the bottleneck is the promotion-mission roll, not rep volume. Use CZs if you're enjoying the combat and banking credits anyway; otherwise courier/transport/donation missions fill the bar faster per minute. Also note the Mandalay is a traveller, not a brawler — for any serious Medium/High zone work, bring the Python Mk II or Cobra Mk V combat builds, not the explorer.

08

Massacre Missions Inside CZs

Stacking works — but only across different mission-givers.

"Kill Faction X Ships" missions are completed cleanly in a CZ where X is a combatant: side against X, and X's ships are your legal kills. Three rules govern how the tally fills:

  • Side correctly. Join X's enemy. Kills only count against the faction you're not fighting for.
  • Spec Ops don't count. Ships spawned by the Spec Ops objective never tick combat-bond/massacre missions — kill the regular combatants for mission credit.
  • Stacking is per mission-giver. One kill counts toward multiple missions only if they were issued by different factions. Two missions from the same faction targeting X do not share a kill — you'd grind the full combined total.
// How to actually stack

Collect "Kill X" missions against one target faction from as many different Imperial mission-givers as possible (across stations / nearby systems). Then each qualifying kill ticks one mission from every different giver at once. For same-giver missions, kills tick the oldest accepted mission first, then the next.

// Tracking aid

The EDMC Massacres plugin (for Elite Dangerous Market Connector) groups accepted massacre missions by giver, shows total kills needed per faction, and flags your largest stack — worth running if you lean into combat farming.

09

Winning the War (BGS)

Individual zone wins ladder up into the war's outcome.

Combat zones are a Background Simulation (BGS) tool. Here's how a zone win becomes a war win:

  • Zone victories and completed war-missions feed a daily tally determining which side "won the day."
  • The faction that wins the most days takes Influence when the war resolves; the loser sheds Influence (and can lose control of assets/stations).
  • During a war the embattled factions' Influence is frozen — combat bonds are effectively the only lever on the outcome until it ends.
  • Check who won the previous day on the faction's station news page, or by expanding the faction in the Status tab of your internals panel.

For pure rep/credit grinding the war outcome doesn't matter to you — but if you ever want to flip a station to a friendlier faction (handy for cheaper outfitting or unlocking access), this is the mechanism.

10

Powerplay Conflict Zones

A distinct zone type that pays merits — not the same as a BGS war.

Under Powerplay 2.0 there are dedicated Power Conflict Zones, separate from ordinary faction-war CZs. They look similar but differ in important ways:

  • They reward Powerplay merits — ordinary CZs do not. Credits may be a touch lower, but merits drive your Powerplay rating.
  • You're auto-assigned to the Power you're pledged to. If neither side is your Power, you may choose freely.
  • Power bonds are issued under the Power's literal name (e.g. a named Power) and redeemed at the Combat Bond Contact on that Power's Stronghold Carrier, not a normal station.
  • As of the most recent design they appear only in the Medium variety.
// Note vs your Lavigny-Duval standing

You're currently Hostile to A. Lavigny-Duval, whose Powerplay presence covers systems like Cemiess. Power CZs are a separate consideration from your Empire rank grind — you don't need to touch Powerplay to reach Squire. File this section under "good to know," not "to-do."

11

On-Foot Conflict Zones

Infantry warfare — reinforcement attrition and capture points.

Accessed on foot at a settlement, ideally via Frontline Solutions (which lets you respawn back into the fight). Intensity (Low/Med/High) can't be read in-ship; check the planetary map's threat number or Frontline's listing.

How a battle is won

  • Two teams of 12 fighters, dropped in by dropship. Each team starts with 1,000 reinforcement points and loses 5 every time one of its members is killed or critically injured.
  • Reduce the enemy to 0 points and eliminate the remaining members to win.
  • Secondary objective — control points (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta, Echo, Foxtrot; max three active at once): stand in the zone until the meter hits 100%. Capturing one strips 50 reinforcement points from the enemy and pays every capturing player a 3,000 CR bond.

Pay & gear

  • Per-kill bonds run a few thousand up to tens of thousands; a strong High zone can net ~5–20M in one session.
  • Mercenary rank matters: climbing it (e.g. Defenceless → Soldier) roughly doubles payout. Engineered suit (Maverick/Dominator) and weapons make a large difference.
  • Redeem at Frontline Solutions or a Combat Voucher Contact in the concourse.
// Reset trick

Entering the zone manually (not via the Frontline dropship) lets you reset it quickly by jumping to supercruise and returning — useful for chaining fresh zones, at the cost of losing the easy respawn.

12

Ship & Loadout Doctrine

Match the hull to the tier, and fly to bank rather than to brag.

  • Engineering by tier: Low needs little; Medium wants a solid engineered build; High demands a well-engineered ship or a player wing. NPCs scale to match.
  • Weapons: don't run pure beam lasers for long engagements — heat and sustain become a problem. Mix sustained energy with kinetic (multi-cannons) so you're not fully dependent on either; bring enough ammo for an extended fight.
  • Defence: shield tank with resistance-augmented boosters favours the long, multi-ship slog of a CZ over a glass-cannon gank build. Carry an AFMU for module repair on extended sessions.
  • Wingman trick: "Select Highest Threat" tends to align allied NPCs onto your target — let them tank while you DPS.
  • Don't over-gun a soft zone: a monster build in a Low/Medium triggers extra enemy Spec Ops wings.
// From the fleet roster

Python Mk II (KA-05P) — your medium combat flagship; the natural High-zone choice. Cobra Mk V (KA-05C) — capable for Low/Medium. Mandalay — leave it parked for CZ work; it's a long-range traveller with no business in a sustained warzone. Standard combat doctrine applies here: G5 Overcharged plant with Thermal Spread, G5 Dirty Drives, Thermal Vent beams, a Corrosive multi-cannon, Heavy Duty boosters/HRPs.
