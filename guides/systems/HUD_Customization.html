Field Briefing

It's a nine-number text file, not a settings screen.

Elite has no in-game HUD colour option. Instead, every pixel of the ship HUD is pushed through a 3×3 colour-transform matrix defined in a plain XML file. Edit nine numbers and the whole interface re-tints — for free, client-side, and with Frontier's blessing. The catch: the matrix is global, so it recolours friend/foe markers, shields, warnings and even commander portraits along with the pretty bits. This manual covers the maths, the exact procedure, a set of copy-paste presets, and the modern shader mod (EDHM) that breaks past the matrix's limits to recolour elements individually.

01

What is the HUD?

Concept

The HUD (Heads-Up Display) is the entire holographic interface projected across your cockpit canopy — the gauges, panels and markers the game draws on top of the world. In lore it's the ship's interface; in practice it's the layer you read to fly, fight, trade and navigate.

The ship HUD is built from one persistent overlay plus three pull-out panels you summon with the directional keys/stick. Knowing the panels cold is a survival skill — being able to instantly re-route power or pick an escape vector can save a rebuy. The breakdown below follows the NEWP HUD guide's anatomy.

The main flight overlay

Comms & info (top-left/top)
Chat, mission and NPC messages, friend/wing requests; the GMT game clock and new-contact alerts. NPC interdiction warnings appear here first.
Status strip
Powerplay standing, your ship's signature (noise — more power/heat = easier to lock from range), fuel reserve, and mass-lock / landing-gear / cargo-scoop indicators.
PIPs / power distributor
The SYS / ENG / WEP capacitor balance. Shields, thruster output and weapon supply respectively; RST resets to 2-2-2.
Shields & hull
The three rings around your ship silhouette are shield layers; once down, damage eats hull (the percentage bar beneath).
Radar / scanner
3D contact display. Filled = NPC, hollow = player; green = your faction, yellow = neutral, red = hostile; square = ship, triangle = hardpoints deployed. Heat gauge left, speed/blue optimal-turn band right.
Target panels
Locked-target shields/hull (right), plus the target/location info block — name, ship, distance, allegiance, or system info when nothing is locked.

The three pull-out panels

  • Navigation panel (left) — system contacts, the Transactions tab (active missions, bounties, fines), the Contacts tab (request docking here), and the Target sub-system tab for module sniping. Houses the galaxy-map and system-map buttons — both worth a dedicated keybind.
  • Comms panel (top) — chat, inbox, social/friends, history, squadron feed, and the text-channel settings.
  • Systems / ship panel (right) — commander info and rebuy cost, the Modules tab (toggle modules and set power priority), Fire Groups, ship options, inventory (cargo/materials), faction status, and Galnet radio.
Reference
The fullest plain-English walkthrough of every panel and indicator is NEWP's HUD guide: newp.io/hud. This manual is about re-skinning that interface, not learning to read it.
02

Why you'd care

Concept

"Orange fatigue" is a real and widely-shared complaint. Beyond aesthetics, recolouring the HUD has practical and accessibility payoffs.

Comfort & aesthetics

Break the monotony of the default amber after hundreds of hours.
Match a personal palette, a squadron identity, or a ship's livery.
Cooler tints (blue/teal/green) read as easier on the eyes for long sessions — and in VR, where bright orange can fatigue.

Legibility & accessibility

Higher contrast against bright skyboxes, station interiors or planet surfaces.
Colour-blindness: the default relies on a red/yellow/green contact scheme that can be hard to separate — a custom matrix can widen those differences.
Cleaner, more readable screenshots and video.
The trade-off
Because the standard method recolours everything uniformly, a careless palette can do the opposite of help — washing out red danger warnings or blurring the green-vs-red friend/foe distinction. Good customization is as much about preserving signal as changing colour. More on this in §03 and §08.
03

What you can (and can't) change

Concept

There is no setting for HUD layout. You cannot move, resize, hide or re-order panels and gauges — the geometry is fixed. What you can change is colour, and how much control you get depends on the method.

With the built-in XML matrix (vanilla, no mods)

  • You change: the colour of the entire ship HUD at once — panels, gauges, text, radar, the lot — by transforming the game's source colours.
  • It also affects, like it or not: shield/contact colours, friend/foe markers, warning text, the galaxy & system map, and commander avatar portraits (these always shift, sometimes dramatically).
  • You cannot: recolour one element independently of the rest. Every HUD pixel runs through the same matrix. You also can't change layout, fonts or sizes.

With the EDHM shader mod (advanced)

  • Per-element colour — set distinct colours for individual HUD components instead of one global transform, avoiding the "everything turns one colour" problem.
  • Cabin lighting — recolour (even bi-colour) the cockpit ambient light, and dim the dashboard lights that glare into your eyes.
  • In Odyssey, EDHM exposes RGB values for many individual HUD/panel elements; in Horizons you can blend XML colours with EDHM's set.
The short version
Want a different colour with zero install and zero risk? Edit the XML. Want surgical, per-element control and cabin lighting? Use EDHM. Both are covered below.
04

How the colour matrix works

Concept

The game draws the HUD in a base palette built around its signature "star orange." Before display, every colour is multiplied by a 3×3 transform you supply. Understanding the maths means you can read and write presets by hand instead of guessing.

Each output channel is a weighted mix of the three source channels. The three XML lines are the rows of the matrix:

Default = identity (orange, unchanged)
from Rfrom Gfrom B
MatrixRed100
MatrixGreen010
MatrixBlue001
Each row passes its own channel through untouched → no change. This is the baseline orange HUD.
Example = orange → blue
from Rfrom Gfrom B
MatrixRed000
MatrixGreen010
MatrixBlue100
Red output is zeroed; the strong source-red of orange is redirected into the blue channel. Green is kept. Orange elements become blue.

Read mathematically, for a source pixel (R,G,B):

The transformoutput = Matrix × source
# MatrixRed   = r1, r2, r3   →   out.R = r1·R + r2·G + r3·B
# MatrixGreen = g1, g2, g3   →   out.G = g1·R + g2·G + g3·B
# MatrixBlue  = b1, b2, b3   →   out.B = b1·R + b2·G + b3·B
  • Values aren't capped at 0–1. You can use values above 1 to boost intensity and negative values to subtract a channel — many sharp presets use numbers like 1.75 or -1 to push saturation or carve out a hue. (Some old reports note negatives behaved oddly in early Horizons; they work in current builds.)
  • Keep warnings legible. The danger reds and info blues run through the same matrix. The clever "purple HUD that keeps red warnings" presets work by leaving a small amount of red in MatrixRed rather than zeroing it.
  • This is why web editors exist — eyeballing nine numbers against a live preview beats blind trial-and-error. See §07.
05

How to change it — step by step

Hands-on

The vanilla method: edit one file, restart the game. Always edit GraphicsConfigurationOverride.xml, never the base GraphicsConfiguration.xml — the Override survives game patches and a mistake there is far less likely to break anything.

  1. Close Elite Dangerous entirely. The file is read at launch.
  2. Find the file. On Windows it lives at:
    File location · WindowsGraphicsConfigurationOverride.xml
    %LOCALAPPDATA%\Frontier Developments\Elite Dangerous\Options\Graphics\
    Paste that path (including %LOCALAPPDATA%) into the Explorer address bar. If your Windows profile is relocated to another drive, it resolves under that profile's AppData\Local instead of C:. macOS: ~/Library/Application Support/Frontier Developments/…/Options/Graphics.
  3. If the file doesn't exist, create it as a plain-text file with exactly that name (watch for a hidden .txt extension), and paste the default block below into it.
  4. Back it up first. Copy the file (or the default block) somewhere safe before editing. This is the one-line insurance policy everyone recommends.
  5. Edit the matrix. Inside <GUIColour> → <Default>, replace the three <Matrix…> lines with your chosen values (from a preset or a web editor's output).
  6. Save, close, launch. The new colours appear the moment you're in the cockpit — no further toggles.
Default / reset block — paste this to start (or to revert)UTF-8 · no BOM
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8" ?>
<GraphicsConfig>
  <GUIColour>
    <Default>
      <LocalisationName>Standard</LocalisationName>
      <MatrixRed> 1, 0, 0 </MatrixRed>
      <MatrixGreen> 0, 1, 0 </MatrixGreen>
      <MatrixBlue> 0, 0, 1 </MatrixBlue>
    </Default>
  </GUIColour>
</GraphicsConfig>
To revert
Restore the three matrix lines to 1,0,0 / 0,1,0 / 0,0,1, or simply delete the whole <GUIColour>…</GUIColour> block (or the Override file itself). Default orange returns on next launch.
Common failure
If colours don't change: you edited the base file instead of the Override; the file has a hidden .txt extension; the XML is malformed (a missing tag or stray character makes the game ignore it); or the game was still running when you saved. Re-check those four.

Tools that do it for you

Hands-on

Two families: web matrix editors (free, no install, generate the XML from sliders against a live preview) and EDHM (an installed shader mod with per-element control). Web editors are the safe default; EDHM is the power option.

Arkku's HUD editor

arkku.com/elite/hud_editor
The community's long-standing reference editor. Drag sliders against a HUD screenshot, copy the generated XML from the box, paste it over your three matrix lines. Works for both Horizons and Odyssey's ship HUD. The tool most preset links point back to.

An alternative slider editor with a "Copy XML" button. Iodred's CodePen fork adds keyboard controls for faster experimentation. Pick whichever editor's preview you trust most — they all output the same XML format.

EDHM-UI

Mod · Shader
PC onlyPer-elementThemes
github.com/BlueMystical/EDHM_UI
The serious option. EDHM (originally by CMDR Georg, now community-maintained) uses the 3Dmigoto shader injector to recolour individual HUD elements, change cabin lighting (incl. bi-colour) and dim glare. EDHM-UI by CMDR Blue Mystic wraps it in a graphical app with bundled themes — no manual config files. Horizons & Odyssey. Strictly graphical; it doesn't touch game mechanics or server data. EDProfiler

Tool · Profiles

Manages graphics profiles you can apply before launch — handy if you want one HUD/look for a monitor and another for VR. It writes to the same GraphicsConfigurationOverride.xml, so it plays nicely with the matrix method.
Recommended path
Start with Arkku or NEWP to dial in a colour and confirm the procedure. If you later hit the "everything's one colour / warnings washed out" wall, graduate to EDHM-UI for per-element control. EDHM is widely regarded as one of the most worthwhile QoL mods for the game.
08

Odyssey notes & gotchas

Hands-on

Things specifically worth knowing on PC / Odyssey before and after you change anything.

  • Is it allowed / will it get me banned? No. The effect is purely client-side; Frontier has stated it has no problem with HUD colour mods and has even featured customised HUDs. The XML edit touches no game mechanic and no server data. EDHM is likewise a strictly graphical mod.
  • On-foot stays orange. The XML matrix transforms the ship HUD. In Odyssey, on-foot terminals and much of the suit HUD largely keep the standard orange/white and aren't recoloured by the matrix. EDHM's Odyssey version reaches more of these elements.
  • Edit the Override, not the base file. GraphicsConfiguration.xml is overwritten on patches and a mistake can cause issues; GraphicsConfigurationOverride.xml persists across updates and is the safer target. (EDProfiler also writes here.)
  • Commander portraits always shift. Avatar/mission-giver pictures are tinted by the same matrix — expect them to look "off" with strong palettes. There's no way to exempt them with the XML method.
  • Friend/foe and warnings are at stake. Test your palette against a real contact (a hollow vs filled, green vs red marker) and a warning before relying on it in combat. If reds or the contact colours become ambiguous, dial back. This is the single most important safety check.
  • VR: cooler/dimmer palettes noticeably reduce eye fatigue; EDHM's cabin-light dimming helps further with headset glare.
  • After a big update, re-launch and glance at the HUD — the Override should persist, but verify, and keep your backup/preset handy just in case.
Same file, more tricks
The GraphicsConfigurationOverride.xml file is also where the community enables other client-side tweaks — e.g. galaxy-map/route colours and skybox/nebula density adjustments. Out of scope here, but worth knowing the one file does double duty.
