CombatRole & Activities

A full deep-dive on the combat role in Elite Dangerous — not which ship to fly (that lives in the companion ship dossiers), but what fighting actually is: the engine mechanics under the hood, the venues you fight in, how to start, what to do mid- and end-game, and every reward and system the role plugs into.

The role in one line

Combat is a family of activities sharing one engine: strip the shield with thermal, break the hull with kinetic, manage pips and heat, and don't die. Master that loop once and it pays you in credits, combat rank, faction influence, Powerplay merits, engineering materials, and the Navy ranks that gate your endgame ships.

01

What the Combat Role Actually Is

"Combat" is not a single activity but a cluster of them, all running on the same flight-and-gunnery engine. The thing that unites bounty hunting, conflict zones, massacre missions, piracy and Powerplay fighting is that you point guns at another ship and apply damage faster than it applies damage to you. The differences are the venue, the payout type, and the opposition — not the fundamentals.

The minute-to-minute loop is always the same four beats:

1
AcquireScan / interdict / target a hostile
2
Strip shieldThermal damage, 4 pips WEP
3
Break hullKinetic, hold gimbal lock
4
Survive & bankFlip pips to SYS, collect reward

Where it differs from your other fleet roles: trading and exploration reward route optimisation and patience; combat rewards build correctness and pip discipline. A poorly-built combat ship doesn't lose money slowly — it dies and pays a rebuy. That's why the role is so engineering-hungry, and why it sits at the centre of the Ka0s fleet doctrine.

Scope note

Anti-Xeno (Thargoid) combat is a separate role with its own weapons (Gauss, AX multi-cannons, flak), heat and caustic-damage rules. It is covered in its own dossier and is deliberately out of scope here — everything below is human/NPC combat.

02

Core Gameplay Mechanics

Six mechanical systems decide every fight. Internalise these and the activities in §3 become variations on a theme.

2.1 — The damage triangle (the most important thing)

Every fight has two phases — shield, then hull — and they want opposite damage types. This is the single biggest source of "why am I taking forever to kill things."

Damage typeWeaponsvs Shieldsvs Hull
ThermalLasers — beam, burst, pulseStrong (shields are weak to heat)Weak — bounces off armour
KineticMulti-cannons, cannons, frag cannons, railgunsResisted by shieldsStrong — armour is weak to it
ExplosiveMissiles, torpedoes, mines, plasma (part)ResistedStrong vs hull & modules
AbsolutePlasma accelerators (part), some special FXIgnores resistancesIgnores resistances
The lesson the Python taught

A ship that's all kinetic guns (multi-cannons) spends the entire shield phase doing almost nothing, because shields resist kinetic. You need enough thermal to break shields quickly, then your kinetic does the real killing on hull. A balanced bird carries both; an unbalanced one feels "slow to kill" even with big guns.

2.2 — Weapon mounts

  • Fixed — highest DPS and best projectile speed, but you must aim. Rewards skill; the PvP standard.
  • Gimballed — auto-track inside a cone. Lower DPS, easiest to land, but chaff breaks the lock. The PvE workhorse.
  • Turreted — track almost any angle, lowest DPS, fire-and-forget. Best on big slow hulls and for gunner-crewed fighters.

2.3 — The power distributor & pips

You have six pips to distribute across three capacitors, max four in any one:

SYS
Shield resistance + recharge rate. Flip here when taking fire.
ENG
Engines top speed + boost frequency. Range control.
WEP
Weapons fire without the capacitor draining. 4 here while shooting.

The combat rhythm is pip-flipping: 4 pips to WEP while you're on target and dealing damage, snap to 4-SYS the instant an enemy is hammering your shield. Doing this well is worth more than any single module upgrade.

2.4 — Heat

Firing and charging generate heat. Above 100% your ship takes thermal damage; above ~150% modules start to break. Heat sink launchers dump heat instantly; the Thermal Vent experimental on beam lasers dumps your heat into the target every time you fire — the standard fix for hot energy builds. Running cold also makes you harder to target and lets you hide (silent running).

2.5 — Subsystem targeting

Sub-target an enemy's modules to cripple rather than just delete: kill the Power Plant (forces a malfunction or outright kill), Drives (immobilise), FSD (stop it fleeing), or Power Distributor (starve its guns and shields). Cannons and railguns with high per-shot damage are best for this; it's how you stop a fleeing assassination target or disable a piracy mark without destroying its cargo.

2.6 — Defences & utilities

Survival modules

  • Shields — Bi-Weave (fast regen, lower buffer) for sustained PvE; Reinforced/Prismatic (big buffer) for spikes and PvP.
  • Shield Boosters — Heavy Duty for raw buffer; Resistance Augmented to plug resist holes.
  • SCBs — Shield Cell Banks instantly restore a chunk; ammo-limited and generate heat.
  • HRP / MRP — Hull and Module Reinforcement Packages; your second life when shields drop.

Utility mounts

  • Chaff — breaks enemy gimbal/turret lock for a few seconds. Huge survivability.
  • Heat Sink — instant heat dump; also escapes some lock-ons.
  • Point Defence — shoots down incoming missiles & hatch-breaker limpets.
  • Kill Warrant Scanner (KWS) — reveals bounties from other jurisdictions and stacks the payout. Near-mandatory for bounty hunting.

2.7 — NPC rank & the opposition

NPCs carry their own combat rank ladder — Harmless → Mostly Harmless → Novice → Competent → Expert → Master → Dangerous → Deadly → Elite. Higher-ranked NPCs fly better-built ships and pip-flip against you. The reward for a kill scales with the target's danger, so a Deadly Anaconda is both harder and far more lucrative than a Harmless Sidewinder.

Fleet doctrine reminder

The Ka0s combat engineering pattern: G5 Overcharged power plant (Thermal Spread), G5 Overcharged multi-cannons, exactly one Corrosive Shell multi-cannon per ship (it debuffs the target so all your weapons hit harder), Auto Loader on the rest, Dirty Drives thrusters, Heavy Duty boosters & HRPs. Apply experimentals last, in a single in-person engineer visit.

Powerplay caveat (from the playbook)

Only combat-type Powerplay actions bank merits — kills in contested space. Passenger missions earn zero Powerplay merits under any Power. The recommended pledge is Jerome Archer (Federation, combat ethos): bounty hunting in his territory simultaneously doubles bounty credits, banks merits, and advances the Tod McQuinn unlock.

04

Getting Started — Early Game

The goal of the early game is to learn the loop cheaply, in a hull you can afford to rebuy, before you sink engineering into anything.

  1. Pick a cheap, tough small hull. A small-pad combat ship — the Viper Mk IV in the fleet — is fast, durable and cheap to rebuy. Don't engineer it yet; learn in it.
  2. Fit the bounty-hunting kit. A mix of thermal (a couple of pulse/burst lasers) and kinetic (multi-cannons), a Bi-Weave shield, two shield boosters, a chaff launcher, and a Kill Warrant Scanner. The KWS pays for itself in one session.
  3. Go to a Low or Standard RES. Drop into a ringed planet's RES; system security ships fight alongside you. Let them tank while you learn to land shots and flip pips.
  4. Scan before you shoot. Target a ship, scan it — only engage WANTED ships, or you'll earn a bounty on your own head. KWS-scan to stack the voucher value.
  5. Practise pip discipline. 4 WEP on the attack, snap to 4 SYS when shields drop. Chaff when a gimballed NPC is tearing you up. Boost away to reset if you're losing.
  6. Bank often. Bounty vouchers are only paid when redeemed at a station (or any Interstellar Factors contact). Don't hoard a fortune in a ship that can be destroyed.
Don't die broke

An undestroyed ship carries unredeemed vouchers; a destroyed one loses them. Cash out every couple of runs, and never fly a fit you can't afford to rebuy. Respawn happens at the nearest rebuy-capable port to where you died — including a fleet carrier — not your last dock.

05

Mid-Game Activities

The mid-game is where combat stops being "can I win this fight" and becomes "how fast can I clear the site." Three things change: you engineer, you move to a medium hull, and you start stacking rewards.

Engineer the guns and the tank

Run the fleet pattern: Felicity Farseer (FSD, done), then Tod McQuinn for G5 Overcharged multi-cannons, a G5 Overcharged power plant with Thermal Spread, Dirty Drives, and Heavy Duty boosters/HRPs. Engineer the module, not the ship — an engineered gun keeps its mods when you move it between hulls, so the spend is fleet-portable.

Graduate to a medium gun platform

Step up to a medium-pad flagship — the fleet's Python Mk II — which carries roughly 84% of a Corvette's hardpoint tonnage but lands on a medium pad and rebuys for far less. This is the workhorse for High RES and medium-intensity CZs.

Start stacking rewards

  • Massacre mission stacking — pull multiple kill missions targeting the same anarchy faction from a hub system, then farm the kills once. Every kill ticks several missions at once.
  • Conflict zone bond farming — pick the winning side, focus high-value targets and specialists, and turn bonds in to the controlling faction for the influence bonus.
  • Rank missions — combat reputation surfaces Federal/Imperial Navy missions; this is where the long Navy-rank grind toward the capstone ships actually happens (see §9).
Throughput beats alpha

Mid-game earnings are a kills-per-hour game, not a damage-per-shot game. Exploiting allied security/wing ships to skip the shield phase, clean pip discipline, and not chasing fleeing targets matter more than a marginally bigger gun.

06

End-Game Activities

End-game combat is about sustained throughput in the hardest venues and the prestige ladders.

  • High-intensity Conflict Zones — the densest combat-bond farm in the game for a fully-engineered large hull. A geared ship clears bonds continuously; thin hulls get deleted.
  • The capstone gunship — the Federal Corvette (Rear Admiral-gated, ~187M CR) or Imperial Cutter as the tanky alternative. The whole Federal thesis builds toward this.
  • Combat Elite — grinding the combat rank to Elite (and Elite I–V beyond) is a long-tail prestige goal, achieved naturally through everything above.
  • Top-tier Powerplay — high merit standing unlocks Power-specific modules (e.g. prismatic shields, Powerplay weapons) that feed straight back into your combat builds.
  • PvP & Community Goal combat — the skill ceiling: FA-off, fixed-weapon convergence, min-maxed defensive fits.
Where the fleet is pointed

The end-game combat arc for CMDR Ka0s is explicit: finish the engineering spend across the combat ships, grind Federal Navy rank to Rear Admiral, and stand up the Corvette as the large-pad flagship — with the Python Mk II remaining the medium-pad daily driver.

09

How Combat Interacts With Other Systems

Combat is not a silo — it's the hub the rest of the progression wheel turns on.

Engineering ↔ Combat

A circular dependency: engineered guns and tanks make combat fast and survivable, and combat (kills, threat USS, HGEs) drops the manufactured materials and encoded data that engineering consumes. Tod McQuinn's unlock is itself a combat task.

Powerplay ↔ Combat

Pledging a combat Power (Jerome Archer) turns bounty hunting into a triple-dip: credits, merits, and unlock progress. Merit rewards then loop back as combat modules.

BGS & Conflict Zones

CZs only appear when factions are at war, and that war state is driven by BGS activity. Handing in bonds tips the war — combat is the most direct way to move a system's balance of power.

Navy Ranks ↔ Endgame Ships

Combat reputation surfaces Federal/Imperial Navy missions; Navy rank gates the capstone hulls. Combat is both the means (rep, credits) and the motive (the Corvette) for the rank grind.

Materials & Trading

Combat funds the whole fleet and supplies engineering mats; piracy bridges directly into the trading economy (you still have to fence the cargo). Raw-material shortfalls are solved at the trader, not in a RES.

On-Foot (Odyssey)

Ground Conflict Zones and settlement raids extend the combat role to infantry play, with their own suit/weapon engineering and their own merit and influence payouts.

10

Field Notes

  • Carry both damage types. The single most common "slow to kill" cause is an all-kinetic or all-thermal fit. Break shields with thermal, hull with kinetic.
  • One Corrosive Shell, never two. The debuff doesn't stack usefully — one multi-cannon with Corrosive Shell boosts all your weapons against that target; everything else runs Auto Loader.
  • Pip-flipping is free DPS and free tank. No module upgrade matches the value of disciplined 4-WEP / 4-SYS swapping.
  • Let allies eat the shield phase. In Low/Std RES and CZs, let security or your faction's wing strip shields, then you cash the hull-phase kill. Throughput, not heroics.
  • KWS pays the rent. A Kill Warrant Scanner stacks bounty value across jurisdictions — never bounty-hunt without one.
  • Bank vouchers and bonds often. They're only worth anything redeemed; a rebuy wipes the unbanked ones.
  • Sub-target to control the fight. FSD to stop a runner, Drives to pin, Power Plant to finish — high-alpha cannons and rails do this best.
  • Engineer modules, not ships. Mods travel with the module across the fleet, so combat spend is portable — build for reuse.
Bottom line

Combat is the engine room of the whole fleet plan: it earns the credits, drops the materials, banks the merits, and grinds the rep that everything else — engineering, Powerplay, the Corvette — runs on. Get the four-beat loop and pip discipline right, and every other reward track advances on autopilot.
