01

Role & Overview

The Hauler is Zorgon Peterson's bargain-basement freighter, and its exploration party trick is mass — or the lack of it. At 14 tonnes it's the lightest ship in the game, and since jump range scales inversely with mass, even a modest FSD throws it a remarkable ~50 LY engineered. For a hull that costs about as much as a single rebuy on a real explorer, that's extraordinary.

The catch is everything else. Its six tiny optionals barely fit a small scoop, a scanner and an AFMU, with no room for redundancy or an SRV bay worth the name; its shields are minimal and it's slow in normal flight. The Hauler is a stunt and a statement: proof that range is about mass, not money. It's a novelty first explorer, an ultra-cheap expendable scout, or simply a fun demonstration of how far almost nothing can take you.

Where this hull shinesUltra-budget and novelty exploration: a near-free first jump into the black, expendable scouting, range-on-a-shoestring challenges, and proving that lightness beats spending.

Four things make the Hauler a novelty explorer:

  • The lightest hull in the game. 14 tonnes means even a small FSD delivers ~50 LY — range out of all proportion to its size and price.
  • Almost free. At ~30k Cr it costs less than a rebuy on a real explorer — the cheapest path to deep space, period.
  • Small-pad access. Lands at any outpost, so resupply on the way out and back is never blocked.
  • A pure mass lesson. It proves the core exploration truth — range comes from shedding mass, not spending credits.
The ceiling, stated honestlyIts six tiny optionals carry only the barest kit — a small scoop, scanner and a single AFMU, with no redundancy and barely an SRV — and its shields and fuel tank are minimal, so long jump chains and self-sufficient expeditions are out. It's slow, fragile and cramped. The Hauler is a stunt and an ultra-budget scout, not a ship for a serious long expedition.

At ~30k Cr the Hauler is almost free, and even a fully A-rated, lightly engineered explorer fit stays around 3M Cr all-in — the cheapest deep-space ship possible, by a wide margin.

It's a pure novelty-and-value pick. Spend a little more on a Diamondback Scout or DBE for a vastly better explorer; choose the Hauler only for the rock-bottom price, an expendable scout, or the fun of proving how far nothing can go.

Deep space for pocket changeA long-range explorer for around 3M Cr all-in — and a near-free 30k hull. Nothing reaches the black for less. The Diamondback Scout is the sensible upgrade.

07

Initial Loadout — Buy-Only Plan

Buy the hull (~30k Cr) and fit a class-3 scoop and A-rated FSD — range is instant given the low mass.

Add a Guardian booster, scanner and a single AFMU; keep everything as light as possible.

Strip everything non-essential; the Hauler's whole virtue is its lightness.

08

A-Rated Loadout — Upgrade Plan

A-rating priority for a featherweight explorer:

  • 1FSD → A — On so light a hull, the A-rated FSD yields huge range; do it first. ~150k Cr
  • 2Fuel Scoop → A (class 3) — Refuel between the short jump chains its small tank allows. ~600k Cr
  • 3Guardian FSD Booster 3 — A flat range bonus — a Guardian-site run. material cost
  • 4Detailed Surface Scanner — Cartographic payouts. ~250k Cr
  • 5AFMU — One self-repair unit for the trip. ~100k Cr
Mass is the only leverEverything on the Hauler is about staying light — A-rate and lightweight-engineer the cores, fit the bare minimum, and watch a 30k-credit hull out-jump ships worth millions.

Field Notes — What Else To Know

  • Keep it light — the Hauler's only virtue is mass; don't load it down.
  • Plan short jump chains and frequent scooping; its tiny tank limits range between refuels.
  • Carry one AFMU and accept there's no redundancy — this is a stunt hull, not a long-haul ship.
  • For a real cheap explorer, spend a little more on a Diamondback Scout or DBE.
VerdictThe Hauler is the featherweight bargain of deep space: 14 tonnes of hull that jumps ~50 LY for the price of a rebuy, proving that range is about mass, not money. It carries almost nothing and can't sustain a serious expedition — but as the cheapest possible ticket to the black, and a delightful demonstration of physics over price, it has a charm all its own.
