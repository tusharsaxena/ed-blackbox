01

The Passenger Role

Passenger transport means carrying people, not cargo — from bulk sightseeing tours to pampered VIPs — in cabins fitted in place of cargo racks. It splits sharply into two trades, and the right ship depends on which you fly.

  • Bulk economy tourism. Pack in economy cabins and ferry crowds to sightseeing beacons — the Robigo Mines loop is the archetype. Capacity is everything.
  • Luxury & VIP missions. A few first-class or luxury suites carrying wealthy, demanding passengers for huge payouts — cabin class and comfort matter more than count.
  • Long-range sightseeing. Tours to distant landmarks; jump range and a big fuel tank decide how smoothly (and how on-time) you deliver.
  • Wanted / discrete passengers. Some VIPs are hot — a shield and the speed to escape a scan keep the contract (and the payout) intact.
What makes a liner

Cabin capacity and cabin class first — economy for crowds, first/luxury for VIPs. Then comfort (the Saud Kruger liners carry a happiness bonus), jump range for tour length, and a strong shield: a frightened or injured VIP cancels the mission. Speed helps you keep to a schedule and dodge scans.

02

How These Ships Are Scored

The 1–100 passenger rating weighs, in order:

  • Cabin capacity & class fit — both raw berths for bulk work and the ability to fit large first/luxury suites for VIPs.
  • Comfort — the dedicated Saud Kruger liners (Beluga, Orca, Dolphin) carry a passenger-satisfaction edge that raises payouts and lowers complaints.
  • Jump range & tank — long sightseeing tours live or die on reach.
  • Shield & safety — protecting VIPs from harm and scans; a cancelled VIP mission is a total loss.
  • Pad class & cost — mediums reach more destinations; cost sets how freely you operate.

This is a top-heavy field — the dedicated Saud Kruger liners (Beluga, Orca, Dolphin) and the armoured Cutter cluster near the ceiling, while multipurpose hulls and transporters pressed into cabin work trail well behind, pulling the floor down into the mid-50s. The Beluga tops it on capacity-plus-comfort; the Cutter holds more economy berths but lacks the comfort bonus and the liner polish, which is why it scores just below the dedicated ships.

The three cost figures

  • Hull — the shipyard price of the bare ship.
  • A-rated fit (no engineering) — an approximate all-in credit total for a full role-appropriate loadout using the best buyable modules. A ballpark; your module choices swing it.
  • To engineer — engineering costs materials and engineer unlocks, not credits. A fully-engineered ship costs the same credits as its A-rated fit; the extra cost is a materials grind whose tier (Moderate / Heavy / Very heavy) scales with hull size.
The rebuy column

The cost tables also list an approximate rebuy (~5% of insured value) — what you pay each time the ship is destroyed. It is the number that really governs how boldly you can fly.

Where the credits go

The expense is the cabins — especially first-class and luxury suites, which cost a fortune and eat huge slots, but pay many times more per passenger. A bulk economy fit is cheap; a luxury VIP fit is where the money (and the earnings) live. Engineering costs materials, not credits; a liner’s materials tier is Moderate on the small/medium hulls and Heavy on the larges — mostly just FSD range and a shield.

Two practical notes

  • Match cabin class to the mission. Economy cabins for sightseeing crowds; first/luxury for VIP contracts. A wrong-class cabin can’t take the passenger at all — many liners carry a mix to stay flexible.
  • A shield is non-negotiable for VIPs. Hull damage frightens or injures passengers and can void the contract; never run high-value VIPs without a healthy shield and an escape plan.
09

Field Notes & Verdict

  • Two trades, one role. Bulk economy tourism and luxury VIP work want almost opposite fits — many berths of cheap cabins, or a few hugely expensive suites. Decide which you fly before you buy.
  • Comfort is real money. The Saud Kruger liners’ satisfaction bonus raises payouts and cuts complaints — it’s why the Beluga and Orca out-rate the bigger-capacity Cutter for VIP work.
  • Pad class shapes the loop. Robigo-style bulk tourism often ends at outposts, which is exactly why a medium-pad Lynx Highliner can out-earn a Beluga that can’t dock there.
  • Keep to the clock. Timed sightseeing missions reward range and speed; a long tour in a short-legged hull bleeds bonuses.
  • Protect the cargo that complains. Unlike ore, passengers react to danger — fly smooth, shielded and unscanned, and the payouts are among the best in the game.
Verdict

For the definitive passenger ship, buy the Beluga Liner (95). For fast, stylish luxury VIP work, the Orca (88). For the Robigo bulk-tourism loop into outposts, the medium-pad Lynx Highliner (90) is the specialist’s pick — and the Dolphin (80) remains a delightful, near-free way to carry a VIP or two in comfort.
