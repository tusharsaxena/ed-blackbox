01

The Mining Role

Mining is the craft of extracting and refining materials from asteroid rings — and at the high end it is one of the most lucrative activities in the game. The ship must carry the tools, refine on the move, and hold the ore.

  • Laser mining. Mining lasers chip ore from surface deposits — steady, simple income, and the easiest to start.
  • Core (deep) mining. Seismic charges crack motherlode asteroids for high-value cores (Void Opals, Low Temperature Diamonds) — the big money, with the most gear.
  • Sub-surface & surface deposits. Abrasion Blasters and Sub-surface Displacement Missiles for the in-between yields.
  • Hotspot prospecting. Finding rich rings and the best hotspots with the DSS and Pulse Wave Analyser — the prep work that decides the payday.
What makes a miner

Three things at once: cargo for the refined ore, optional-internal slots for a Refinery and one or two Limpet Controllers (collector + prospector), and hardpoints for the mining tools. A tank to survive a pirate while your limpets are out is the fourth. Pure cargo isn’t enough — a miner needs the slots to spend on tools too.

02

How These Ships Are Scored

The 1–100 mining rating weighs, in order:

  • Effective ore capacity — cargo left after a refinery and limpet controllers take their slots; the real measure of a payday.
  • Tool & slot fit — enough hardpoints and optional internals to run a complete laser or core toolkit without compromise.
  • Survivability — shield and hull to ride out a pirate interdiction while limpets are deployed and you’re stationary.
  • Pad class & access — mediums dock at more ring-adjacent outposts; larges trade access for sheer capacity.
  • Cost & specialisation — how much hull it ties up, and whether it can re-role when you’re done mining.

The scale is roster-relative. The Type-11 tops it not on raw cargo — the Cutter and Type-9 hold more — but on being the only hull designed to mine, with exclusive tools and the perfect slot layout. Compare within a class first.

The three cost figures

  • Hull — the shipyard price of the bare ship.
  • A-rated fit (no engineering) — an approximate all-in credit total for a full role-appropriate loadout using the best buyable modules. A ballpark; your module choices swing it.
  • To engineer — engineering costs materials and engineer unlocks, not credits. A fully-engineered ship costs the same credits as its A-rated fit; the extra cost is a materials grind whose tier (Moderate / Heavy / Very heavy) scales with hull size.
The rebuy column

The cost tables also list an approximate rebuy (~5% of insured value) — what you pay each time the ship is destroyed. It is the number that really governs how boldly you can fly.

04

Small-Pad Mining

A handful of small hulls can fit a complete mining loadout — lasers or seismic charges, a Pulse Wave Analyser, a Refinery, a collector and a prospector limpet controller — but only at the cost of the ore hold. They work as cheap trainers: learn prospecting, cracking and refining for almost nothing, then trade up. None earns at the rate of a medium.

Cost & Engineering Reality

A miner’s engineering is light — the mining tools themselves are barely engineered, and the focus is on getting to the ring, surviving it, and getting home. The tour is short and shared across the hulls here.

What you actually grind for

The mining tools (lasers, charges, abrasion blasters) are left mostly unengineered — there is little to gain. The real engineering is the power distributor (so your weapon capacitor sustains the lasers) and a shield to survive interdiction while you sit still in the ring. Engineering costs materials, not credits; a miner’s materials tier is Moderate on the mediums and Heavy–Very heavy only if you fully roll a large.

Two practical notes

  • Slots are a budget. Every optional internal that holds a refinery or limpet controller is one that isn’t holding ore. The dedicated and large hulls win precisely because they have slots to spare after the toolkit is fitted.
  • A laden miner is a pirate magnet. Sitting motionless with a full hold is the most vulnerable you get — a fighter bay (Krait Mk II, Keelback) or a heavy tank (Cutter) is what turns a raid into a non-event.
09

Field Notes & Verdict

  • Effective cargo > max cargo. The headline tonnage assumes every slot is a cargo rack; a real miner sacrifices two or three slots to a refinery and limpet controllers, so judge a hull by what’s left after the toolkit.
  • Core mining is where the money is. Seismic-charge core mining out-earns laser mining many times over — but needs more tools and more skill. The big hulls shine because they fit both styles at once.
  • Hotspot quality decides the payday. The ship matters less than finding a rich double/triple hotspot; prospect with the PWA and DSS before committing a session.
  • Mine where it’s safe, or bring a bodyguard. Quiet rings let a defenceless barge work in peace; busy ones demand a tank or a fighter.
  • The Type-11 is a commitment. It mines better than anything and does little else — worth it only if mining is a real part of your play.
Verdict

If mining is your profession, the Type-11 Prospector (95) is the only hull built for it. For the biggest single-session payday, the Imperial Cutter (92) rules on capacity and tank. For the best all-round value, the Python (90) mines superbly and re-roles when you’re done — and the Krait Mk II (84) is the pick if you want to laser-mine with a fighter watching your back.
