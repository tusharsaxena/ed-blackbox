Every exploration-capable hull, judged for deep-space exploration specifically and ranked on one 1–100 scale. This dossier groups all 14 explorers by pad class, ranks them, breaks down what each costs to buy, A-rate and engineer, and closes with picks for different kinds of explorer.

01

The Exploration Role

Exploration is the long game: leaving the bubble to scan systems, map bodies, hunt exobiology and bank cartographics. The ship requirements are almost the opposite of combat — you optimise for reach and self-sufficiency, not firepower.

  • Honk & map. Discovery Scanner (the “honk”) plus a Detailed Surface Scanner to map high-value bodies. The bread-and-butter income.
  • Exobiology. Odyssey on-foot sampling of biological signals — the highest-paying exploration activity, and it rewards range + a good SRV/disembark workflow.
  • Long-range expeditions. Trips to the galactic core, the rim or named landmarks — here jump range and fuel-tank size decide how smoothly you travel.
  • Road-to-Riches. Curated routes of high-value mapping targets close to the bubble; the standard way to bank a first fortune.
What makes an explorerJump range first, then heat (low heat = safe fuel-scooping and neutron boosting), a clear canopy for honking and landing, and internal room for an AFMU (self-repair), an SRV (planetary work) and the scanners. A big fuel tank turns range into reach.
02

How These Ships Are Scored

The 1–100 exploration rating weighs, in order:

  • Engineered jump range — the single biggest factor; it sets how far each jump takes you and how few you make.
  • Heat profile — cool ships scoop safely and survive neutron/white-dwarf boosting.
  • Fuel tank & reach — how many jumps between stars; big tanks cross sparse regions.
  • Canopy & visibility — quality-of-life for honking, landing and bio-hunting.
  • Internals — room for AFMU, SRV, DSS and a fuel scoop without compromise.
  • Comfort & cost — agility, looks and how little it hurts to lose far from home.

The scale is roster-relative and engineered-state. Class is not a handicap in the number — a small explorer and a large one on the same score are not equal in reach; the small one is simply excellent for its class. Compare within a class first.

The three cost figures

  • Hull — the shipyard price of the bare ship.
  • A-rated fit (no engineering) — an approximate all-in credit total for a full role-appropriate loadout using the best buyable modules. A ballpark; your module choices swing it.
  • To engineer — engineering costs materials and engineer unlocks, not credits. A fully-engineered ship costs the same credits as its A-rated fit; the extra cost is a materials grind whose tier (Moderate / Heavy / Very heavy) scales with hull size.
The rebuy columnThe cost tables also list an approximate rebuy (~5% of insured value) — what you pay each time the ship is destroyed. It is the number that really governs how boldly you can fly.

Large-Pad Exploration

Large explorers exist for one thing: reach. Their enormous fuel tanks let you cross the sparsest regions of the galaxy with the fewest scoops, and their internals swallow every expedition module at once. The price is a large pad, ponderous handling, and a fortune in hull and rebuy.

Cost & Engineering Reality

An explorer lives or dies on its FSD. The engineering tour is short and shared across every hull here — and far lighter than a combat grind.

Why this grind is the easy oneThe only blueprint that truly matters is FSD Increased Range — one engineer (Farseer), one of the first you unlock, and the bulk of your range gain. Everything else is lightweighting to squeeze out a few more light-years. Engineering costs materials, not credits; for explorers the materials tier is Moderate even on the larges, because there are no weapons or hull stacks to roll.

Two practical notes

  • The Guardian FSD Booster is mandatory. A tech-broker unlock that adds a flat range bonus (up to ~+10.5 LY) — the single best non-engineering upgrade an explorer can fit.
  • Strip for range. Lightweight everything, D-rate non-essentials, carry only the fuel you need; every tonne shed is range gained. An AFMU and SRV are the only “heavy” modules worth their mass far from home.
09

Field Notes & Verdict

  • Range is reach, but the tank is endurance. A high-jump small ship can out-jump an Anaconda per hop, yet the Anaconda crosses a sparse region in fewer stops because it carries more fuel. Match the tool to the trip.
  • Heat is a safety system. Cool ships scoop without cooking and survive neutron boosting; a hot ship turns every fuel star into a gamble.
  • Mediums are the sweet spot. They jump like the best, run cool, and dock anywhere — the rational explorer for almost everyone.
  • Bring an AFMU and an SRV. Self-repair and planetary mobility are what make deep expeditions and exobiology viable; they are worth their mass.
  • The rebuy is your safety net far from home. A cheap hull means a careless landing or a hot scoop costs almost nothing — one reason the budget explorers are so beloved.
VerdictFor the best explorer in the game, buy the Mandalay (96). For the deepest expeditions, the Anaconda (94)’s fuel tank rules. For the best value, the Diamondback Explorer (86) delivers huge range for almost nothing — and the Asp Explorer (88) remains the classic everyone should fly at least once.
