01

The Multipurpose Role

A multipurpose ship is the answer to “I don’t want to own and engineer five specialists.” It is a single hull, balanced enough to be re-fitted for whatever tonight’s play is — and good enough at each that you rarely feel the compromise.

  • Mixed-activity sessions. Bounty hunting, then a delivery mission, then a scan of an unexplored system — all without changing ships.
  • One-ship commanders. Players who want a single, beloved hull they engineer once and fly forever.
  • Mission running. The general mission board mixes combat, courier, data and passenger work; an all-rounder clears it all.
  • Re-roling on the fly. Swap cargo racks for shields and guns in an hour at a station — the same hull, a new job.
What makes an all-rounder

Balance, not peaks. Enough hardpoints to win a fair fight, enough optional internals to haul or fit mining tools, enough jump range to roam, and a hull whose modules you’re happy to re-fit often. A specialist beats it at any one job — but the all-rounder beats owning five specialists.

02

How These Ships Are Scored

The 1–100 multipurpose rating weighs balance across, in rough order:

  • Breadth of internals — the room to fit cargo, mining tools, shields or scanners as the job demands.
  • Firepower — enough hardpoints and distributor to hold its own in combat without a dedicated build.
  • Jump range — the reach to roam and explore between jobs.
  • Flexibility & re-fit cost — how cheaply and quickly it swaps roles, and how few compromises each fit makes.
  • Survivability & handling — a tank and agility that suit every role passably.

The column shows max cargo only as a convenient proxy for internal capacity — do not read it as the verdict. A ship rates highly here for doing several things well, so a lower-cargo hull with great range and guns can out-rank a bigger hold. Compare within a class first.

The three cost figures

  • Hull — the shipyard price of the bare ship.
  • A-rated fit (no engineering) — an approximate all-in credit total for a full role-appropriate loadout using the best buyable modules. A ballpark; your module choices swing it.
  • To engineer — engineering costs materials and engineer unlocks, not credits. A fully-engineered ship costs the same credits as its A-rated fit; the extra cost is a materials grind whose tier (Moderate / Heavy / Very heavy) scales with hull size.
The rebuy column

The cost tables also list an approximate rebuy (~5% of insured value) — what you pay each time the ship is destroyed. It is the number that really governs how boldly you can fly.

Cost & Engineering Reality

The all-rounder’s engineering challenge is breadth: you engineer the core modules once, then keep a stock of role modules (cargo racks, mining tools, extra shields) to bolt on as needed. The core tour is shared across every hull here.

Engineer the core, swap the rest

The beauty of an all-rounder is that the engineered core (FSD, power plant, thrusters, distributor, shield) serves every role — you only swap the cheap, often-unengineered role modules on top. Engineering costs materials, not credits; the materials tier is Moderate for the smalls, Heavy for the mediums, and Very heavy for a fully-rolled Anaconda. Because engineered modules move between your own ships, the work compounds across a fleet.

Two practical notes

  • Keep a module locker. The real superpower is owning a set of role modules — cargo racks, mining tools, a passenger cabin or two — to re-fit the same hull in minutes at a station.
  • A balanced fit beats a min-maxed one here. Resist the urge to specialise; the point of the hull is that it does several things well, so leave room for whatever the next job demands.
09

Field Notes & Verdict

  • A specialist always wins its own game. The all-rounder’s value isn’t beating a dedicated miner at mining — it’s saving you from owning a miner, a trader, a fighter and an explorer all at once.
  • Mediums are the genre’s heartland. They balance guns, internals and range while docking almost anywhere — which is why the Krait pair and the Python define the category.
  • Lean is a choice, not a flaw. Scores a point or two apart often just mean a hull tilts toward combat (Krait Mk II), hauling (Python) or roaming (Mandalay, Asp X). Pick the lean that matches your week.
  • The Cobra Mk V rewrote the small class. A modern small ship now does what used to need a medium — for budget commanders it’s a genuine leap.
  • Engineering compounds. Because modules move between your ships, every core blueprint you roll for an all-rounder pays off across your whole fleet.
Verdict

For the ultimate one-ship fleet, the Anaconda (88) does everything at scale. For the best all-rounder most commanders will ever need, the medium Krait Phantom (86) is the sweet spot — with the Python (85) and Krait Mk II (84) a hair behind on different leans. On a budget, the Cobra Mk V (82) is the finest small all-rounder ever made.
