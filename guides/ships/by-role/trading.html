01

The Trading Role

Trading is the act of moving goods for profit — and at scale it is the most reliable income in the game. The ship’s job is simple: carry as much as possible, to the right kind of station, and arrive in one piece.

  • Bulk commodity loops. Buy low, sell high between fixed markets — profit scales linearly with cargo, so capacity is king.
  • Stacked delivery / wing missions. Take many delivery and source-and-return missions at once; a big hold lets you clear a board in a single run.
  • Rare goods runs. Long loops carrying small quantities of high-value rares — here jump range and speed matter more than raw tonnage.
  • Tritium & fleet-carrier logistics. Feeding a carrier’s fuel and market — pure volume hauling.
What makes a traderCargo capacity first — profit is tonnage × margin. Then pad class: a large pad locks you out of outposts, so medium haulers reach markets larges can’t. Then jump range for route length, and enough shield to survive the interdiction that will come on a profitable lane.
02

How These Ships Are Scored

The 1–100 trading rating weighs, in order:

  • Maximum cargo — the dominant factor; trading profit scales directly with tonnage.
  • Pad class & market reach — mediums dock at outposts that larges cannot; a slight cargo penalty for far wider access.
  • Laden jump range — how far a full hold travels per jump; sets route length and trips-per-hour.
  • Survivability — shield and hull enough to outlast or outrun a pirate interdiction.
  • Speed & cost — faster supercruise = more loops; cheaper hull = lower risk on a defenceless barge.

The scale is roster-relative. A medium scoring 82 is not “worse” than a large scoring 93 — it carries less but reaches markets the large never can. Compare within a class first, then decide whether you need pad reach or raw bulk.

The three cost figures

  • Hull — the shipyard price of the bare ship.
  • A-rated fit (no engineering) — an approximate all-in credit total for a full role-appropriate loadout using the best buyable modules. A ballpark; your module choices swing it.
  • To engineer — engineering costs materials and engineer unlocks, not credits. A fully-engineered ship costs the same credits as its A-rated fit; the extra cost is a materials grind whose tier (Moderate / Heavy / Very heavy) scales with hull size.
The rebuy columnThe cost tables also list an approximate rebuy (~5% of insured value) — what you pay each time the ship is destroyed. It is the number that really governs how boldly you can fly.

Cost & Engineering Reality

A trader’s engineering is light and cheap — you are not min-maxing a weapon platform, just stretching range and shaving a few risks. The tour is short and shared across every hull here.

Where the credits really goFor a trader the big number is the hull and cargo racks, not engineering. Engineering costs materials, not credits, and a trader’s materials tier is light: just FSD range plus a defensive shield roll. The grind tier scales with hull size only because bigger ships have bigger (more numerous) modules to roll — Moderate for the mediums, Very heavy for a fully-rolled Cutter or Panther, but you rarely need to go that far on a hauler.

Two practical notes

  • Shield, don’t armour. A trader’s survival is a shield that buys time to high-wake out of an interdiction — not a slugging match. Fit the biggest bi-weave you can spare a slot for.
  • Laden range is the real range. A full hold tanks your jump distance; engineer the FSD and keep the build light so a loaded run still reaches the next market in a sensible number of jumps.
09

Field Notes & Verdict

  • Pad class is the first decision, not cargo. The most profitable lane is worthless if your large freighter can’t dock at one end of it. Check both stations’ pads before you buy the hull.
  • Profit is tonnage × margin × trips. A faster ship that carries less can out-earn a slow barge by doing more loops — which is exactly why the Cutter rivals the Panther in practice.
  • You will be interdicted. Submit, boost, and high-wake — a shield and good thrusters beat guns for a trader every time.
  • Markets move. Station inventories and prices shift with the background sim; use a live trade tool rather than memorising routes.
  • The rebuy on a defenceless barge stings. A Type-9 is cheap to buy but you fly it knowing one bad interdiction means a real rebuy — price that into the route.
VerdictFor the most cargo in the game, the Panther Clipper Mk II (98). For the best end-game balance of capacity, speed and safety, the Imperial Cutter (95). For the most profit per credit spent, the Type-9 Heavy (93). And if you trade at outposts, the Type-8 Transporter (82) is your real ceiling — the most cargo that still fits a medium pad.
